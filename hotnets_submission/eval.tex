\section{Prototype and Measurements}
\label{sec:eval}

The evidence for tier 1 is a system that exists. We built the full capture-to-advisory stack on a Jetson Orin Nano developer kit, trained a policy in simulation on a desktop machine, deployed it to the device, and validated the loop end to end on simulated drives: prerecorded dashcam footage and a scripted GPS profile pushed through the live pipeline in real time, not an in-vehicle road test. The numbers below show the loop is fast enough and behaves correctly on cheap hardware; they are deliberately not a traffic-flow result, and we close with what would be.

\noindent\textbf{Prototype stack.}
The board runs JetPack~6 with TensorRT~10.3. Detection is a YOLOv8n engine in TensorRT FP16 driven directly through the CUDA runtime~\cite{redmon2016yolo}, partly forced by the platform's CPU-only stock PyTorch. Tracking is a SORT-style associator; distance comes from pinhole geometry with vehicle-width priors rather than a dense depth network, chosen for speed and easy to swap if accuracy becomes the problem~\cite{yang2024depth}. Figure~\ref{fig:camera} shows the detector's forward view on a real dashcam frame. The observation builder implements the vendored 39-field contract of Section~\ref{sec:architecture}; the policy is a two-layer network mirrored in NumPy on the CPU at 0.5\,ms per decision. Ego speed comes from a u-blox USB GPS receiver with hold-on-stale handling (an OBD-II feed is planned as the primary source); a dashboard shows the advisory, and every field is logged with provenance.

\noindent\textbf{Policy training.}
The deployed policy was trained with PPO in a microscopic simulator: one shared policy controlling two vehicles among eight simulated human drivers with varied behavior on a ring, 3{,}000 updates of 256 steps, about ninety minutes per seed on a desktop CPU. Five seeds were trained, one under sensing noise matched to the deployment measurements (100\,m range, 1.5\,m position noise, 0.15\,m/s speed noise). One lesson from training carries over: with a dense speed reward, a one-step crash cost is invisible and the greedy policy collapses to a constant ``fast''; an explicit crash penalty restored gap-conditioned behavior. In simulation the learned policy matches, but does not beat, a hand-designed cooperative-smoothing controller (mean speeds 11.8--12.6 versus 11.1\,m/s), while an uncontrolled stream collapses into stop-and-go under the same demand. The claim is the behavior's shape and edge feasibility, not a simulation leaderboard.

\noindent\textbf{Measured performance.}
Table~\ref{tab:jetson} summarizes the deployed loop. The compute path runs at a 19.8\,ms median unpaced and sustains 29.9\,Hz on paced drives, with p95 latencies of 21.9--25.0\,ms, ten times under our 200\,ms budget. Ego-speed error over a 280\,s drive was 0.121\,m/s RMSE, at the level of the injected noise, even through a scripted 4\,s GPS dropout that the hold-on-stale rule absorbed. The trained policy passed all five pipeline gates (latency, tick rate, GPS freshness, ego-speed error, and perception coverage) on both scenarios. Its advisory behavior, measured after the fact rather than gated, tracked the gap: with a leader within 60\,m it recommended the nominal bin on all 8{,}108 such ticks, on open road it recommended fast on 99.6\% of ticks (the few exceptions had a leader tracked just beyond 60\,m), and it held nominal through 97\% of the low-speed ticks (ego below 12\,m/s) of a hard-braking approach. Its advisory churn was 31 head switches per minute against roughly 170 for a random-weights policy, low enough for a human to follow. Offline replay reproduced all 902 decisions of the braking scenario tick for tick, which makes the safety filter auditable after the fact.

\noindent\textbf{Limitations.}
The dashcam footage is borrowed, so absolute distances can be off by up to about a factor of two, because we had to assume the camera's field of view; lane assignment and the sign of closing speed do not depend on this, and a one-time calibration of the mounted camera removes it. Rear and cooperation fields are currently neutral, so on average almost half of the observation fields are placeholders (mean missingness 0.477) until a rear camera and live peers are added. Advisories still flicker briefly when a leader track first appears near a bin boundary; a small hysteresis would damp this. The driver-facing speed readout currently mirrors the raw decode rather than the filter's gap-aware cap, a display-layer gap we are closing, and an on-road run with a live camera is future work.

\noindent\textbf{Toward a pilot.}
We plan to build from edge feasibility toward a traffic-flow result in stages, each adding cars and realism without touching the road: hardware-in-the-loop replay of many vehicles under swept message loss, delay, sensing range, and compliance; a simulation study that swaps the predecessor's global observation for the local-plus-aggregate contract of Section~\ref{sec:vision}, comparing local-only, aggregate-assisted, and oracle controllers under one safety layer; a closed-course study of whether human drivers can follow the advisories without uncomfortable braking; and, as the endpoint, a Holland-Tunnel-shaped corridor study seeding one instrumented fleet on the feeder lanes of Section~\ref{sec:vision}, which would also exercise the model-based training of Section~\ref{sec:architecture}.
