\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Traffic jams on free-flow highways are not only a capacity problem; they are a distributed stability problem. When local density crosses a critical threshold, small braking events, merge bursts, or overreactions can amplify into persistent stop-and-go waves even without a crash or a fixed bottleneck~\cite{bhardwaj2023understanding,sugiyama2008traffic}. The classical traffic-flow view captures this with the fundamental diagram: flow grows with density until a critical operating point, then collapses as interactions dominate~\cite{greenshields1935study,lighthill1955kinematic,richards1956shock}. Keeping density below that critical point, while holding speed high, keeps the whole stream on the productive branch of the diagram and maximizes sustained network throughput. Small, well-timed local speed adjustments are enough to do this, which is what makes decentralized control a plausible lever on a network-level objective.

Fixed highway controllers are poorly matched to this failure mode. Ramp meters, variable speed limits, and centralized traffic-management systems act from fixed infrastructure and coarse network views; they are valuable where deployed, but expensive to install, unevenly available, and slow relative to local disturbances~\cite{arnold1998ramp,papageorgiou2002freeway}. The vehicle-side actuator, meanwhile, has now been demonstrated twice over. In the field, a single controlled vehicle among roughly twenty human-driven ones damps stop-and-go waves on a ring track~\cite{stern2018dissipation}, and the CIRCLES experiment placed about one hundred controlled vehicles into live interstate traffic, though with purpose-built cars and a centralized speed planner assigning targets over cellular links~\cite{lee2024traffic}. In simulation, a recent self-regulating-cars system showed that the policy can be learned: trained speed modulation on a real highway network improves total throughput by 5\%, reduces average delay by 13\%, and cuts total stops by 3\% relative to no control, applying modulation only at the network's bottleneck segments during peak inflow, with network metrics improving steadily as the fraction of compliant vehicles grows~\cite{self_regulating_cars}. But that system observes global segment state and broadcasts recommendations through a cloud service.

The timing makes deployment worth taking seriously now. Connected and automated vehicles are moving from pilots to revenue service, so the programmable fraction of a highway stream is rising rather than hypothetical. The computation needed to estimate local traffic state is already widely available: robotaxis carry substantial onboard compute, smartphones can run the required perception and inference, and even human-driven vehicles increasingly ship with cameras and onboard computers. A driver-facing advisory rail also already operates at scale in mobile telematics platforms~\cite{cmt2025,samsara2025}. Yet each of these vehicles optimizes only its own trip; the effect each car has on the flow around it is left untapped.

We take the position that the remaining problem is deployment, not algorithms and not radios. The effective control law is coarse: discrete speed and headway bins suffice for a learned policy to improve flow~\cite{self_regulating_cars,stern2018dissipation}. The radio exists: deployed connected-vehicle standards already broadcast per-vehicle safety state at up to ten hertz~\cite{sae2020j2735,etsi2019cam,kenney2011dedicated}, and the aggregate summaries our design consumes are smaller and slower than that. We therefore deliberately do not propose a new vehicle-to-vehicle protocol; we assume a low-rate aggregate exchange rides on radios the community has already built and standardized, and spend the paper on what sits above the substrate: \emph{(i) how little must change in an existing car for it to become self-regulating, and (ii) how few self-regulating cars must be present, and where, for the network to feel them?}

This paper argues that both answers are small, and grounds the argument in a working system. First, it recasts highway congestion control as an edge-deployment problem: ordinary vehicles keep the entire real-time loop on board, act only through bounded advisories, and treat communication as an observability hint rather than a control dependency (Section~\ref{sec:vision}). Second, it gives the bill of changes that makes one car self-regulating: a dashcam-grade camera, an ego-speed feed from GPS or the OBD-II port, a digital twin and policy that fit on phone-class or \$250 embedded compute, and a safety filter, with no connection to the vehicle's controls (Section~\ref{sec:architecture}). Third, it reports a prototype of the recipe on a Jetson Orin Nano that runs a simulation-trained policy against real dashcam footage at a sustained 30\,Hz with 20--25\,ms end-to-end latency (Section~\ref{sec:eval}). Finally, it argues for a minimal deployment model: because congestion is manufactured at bottlenecks, roughly one vehicle per approach lane, about 25 for a corridor like the Holland Tunnel, could regulate the inflow the bottleneck receives, making a single rideshare or delivery fleet a sufficient launch vehicle (Section~\ref{sec:vision}). Section~\ref{sec:discussion} lays out the open questions.
