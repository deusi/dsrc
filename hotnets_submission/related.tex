\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\noindent\textbf{Sparse AVs as traffic actuators.}
Stern et al. showed that a single controlled vehicle can damp stop-and-go waves on a ring track~\cite{stern2018dissipation}, establishing the sparse-actuator premise. Flow and related mixed-autonomy RL work show that learned controllers can improve simulated traffic at low AV penetration~\cite{wu2017flow,vinitsky2018benchmarks,kreidieh2018dissipating}. CIRCLES/MegaVanderTest is the strongest field evidence that controlled vehicles can affect open-road traffic, but it uses purpose-built controlled vehicles and a centralized speed planner that assigns targets over cellular connectivity~\cite{lee2024traffic}. The self-regulating-cars formulation learned network-level speed modulation and quantified its benefit, with a centralized observer and cloud broadcast~\cite{self_regulating_cars}. Our focus is the complement: ordinary vehicles, edge-executed control, and a runtime that survives with no coordinator at all.

\noindent\textbf{Connected-vehicle messaging.}
Deployed connected-vehicle standards have each car broadcast its own position, speed, and heading for collision avoidance at up to ten hertz: the SAE J2735 Basic Safety Message over DSRC and the ETSI Cooperative Awareness Message over C-V2X~\cite{sae2020j2735,etsi2019cam,kenney2011dedicated}. Within the same family, the Collective Perception Service shares per-object detection records~\cite{etsi2023cpm}, and probe-vehicle systems compute neighborhood traffic state by uplinking per-vehicle traces to a backend that aggregates centrally~\cite{seo2017traffic,herrera2010evaluation}. We build on this body of work rather than compete with it: the same radios carry our messages, and we claim no novelty in the substrate. The one semantic difference we rely on is that our unit of exchange is a fixed-size, receiver-side neighborhood aggregate, with no sender addressed, retained, or reconstructable and no backend in the loop, which keeps the privacy surface small and the message size independent of scene complexity.

\noindent\textbf{Infrastructure-centric traffic control.}
Ramp metering, variable speed limits, adaptive signal control, and freeway management systems remain the dominant deployed tools~\cite{arnold1998ramp,papageorgiou2002freeway,scats,scoot}. They are powerful where sensors and actuators exist, but they depend on fixed infrastructure and centralized operational visibility. Self-regulating cars instead treat vehicles as mobile actuators that can be introduced incrementally.

\noindent\textbf{Advisory systems.}
Human-compatible advisory systems show how network objectives can be translated into driver-facing recommendations~\cite{Yan_2023,cho2023temporal,fridman2018human}. This matters because early deployment will be mixed: some vehicles may be automated, some may show dashboard advisories, and many will ignore the system. Mobile telematics platforms show that the driver-facing rail itself already operates at the scale of tens of millions of drivers~\cite{cmt2025,samsara2025}. The open systems question is therefore not only control optimality, but compliance, incentives, and fairness under partial adoption.

\noindent\textbf{Edge AI and networked sensing.}
Embedded perception models and Jetson-class hardware make on-vehicle traffic-state estimation increasingly practical~\cite{nvidia2023jetson,redmon2016yolo,yang2024depth}. Cloud video analytics demonstrates the value of centralized visual processing but also the cost of moving raw data off the edge~\cite{kang2017noscope,kang2018blazeit}. Our communication model takes the opposite position: infer locally, exchange only semantic aggregate state, and keep safety-critical control on the vehicle.
