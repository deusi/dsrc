\begin{abstract}
Highway traffic jams are a congestion-control failure hiding in a transportation system: small local disturbances can push traffic past a critical density, after which stop-and-go waves persist long after the initial burst has disappeared. Prior self-regulating-car systems show that speed modulation can improve flow, but they still steer vehicles from global state broadcast by a cloud coordinator. This paper argues that highway congestion control can move into the traffic stream itself, and that the enabling question is one of communication abstraction. We envision ordinary vehicles as edge nodes that exchange only bounded, semantic, aggregate traffic summaries over a low-rate vehicle-to-vehicle substrate, build ego-centric digital twins of their local road state, and independently compute safety-filtered speed advisories.
The abstraction we propose is neither high-bandwidth perception sharing nor command coordination, and it is distinct from the per-vehicle safety broadcast that existing connected-vehicle standards define; it is a \emph{road-network congestion signal}, a small, stale-aware, lossy indicator of local traffic pressure that remains useful even when only a few vehicles participate.
We specify this communication contract, describe the ego-centric digital twin and the model-based training approach that together support cross-topology adaptation, and outline the safety layer needed for mixed human traffic, grounding edge-compute and bandwidth feasibility with Jetson-class perception measurements. The result is a decentralized, cloud-coordinator-free path toward traffic control that opens several interesting networking challenges.
\end{abstract}
